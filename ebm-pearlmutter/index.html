From Autoregressive Tokens to Energy Landscapes

and the Pearlmutter trick that makes the math cheap

A speculative reconstruction of how Logical Intelligence's Kona might be built — no paper has been published, so this is one architecture that fits the public claims.

1. LLMs commit at every step.

An autoregressive language model factors $p(y \mid x) = \prod_t p(y_t \mid x, y_{<t})$. At each step the head reads the context, produces logits, samples a token, appends it, and continues.

This is fast and it works — but notice what it forbids. Once $y_3 = $ "compute" is sampled, no later activation can rewrite it. If at step 17 the model realizes the right verb at step 3 was "notice", there is no mechanism to revise. The pen never lifts. That structural irreversibility is why chain-of-thought helps so much — it gives the model more left-to-right room to recover from early commitments, because it cannot retract them.

2. The energy-based alternative.

Replace the single forward pass with a learned scalar verifier energy $E_\theta(x, \hat{y})$ whose minima over $\hat{y}$ are good answers to the problem $x$. Here $\hat{y}$ can be a single token, an entire token sequence, an image latent, a grid of values — anything continuous and structured. Generation becomes minimization of the energy in $\hat{y}$-space:

$$ \hat{y}_{k+1} \;=\; \hat{y}_k \;-\; \alpha \,\nabla_{\hat{y}} E_\theta(x, \hat{y}_k), \qquad \hat{y}_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \tag{1} $$

Add Gaussian noise to (1) and you have Langevin dynamics, the sampling version of the same update. For a sequence answer, $\hat{y} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}$ and every position updates simultaneously each step — the picture in Figure 2.

Note: some EBM approaches train $E_\theta$ contrastively — score matching, denoising score matching, noise-contrastive estimation — without ever backpropping through the inference loop. This post covers the unrolled-inference route, which is what makes the Pearlmutter machinery in §5 necessary in the first place.

3. Training the verifier.

Sample $(x, y)$ from data, run (1) for $N$ steps to produce $\hat{y}_N$, apply a loss $J$ between $\hat{y}_N$ and the target $y$, backprop through every step:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; J\!\left(\hat{y}_N(\theta; x),\, y\right) \tag{2} $$

$J$ is the only place the target's type enters:

  • Continuous targets (matrix completion, image latents): MSE.
  • Discrete tokens: categorical cross-entropy.
  • VAE features: Smooth-L1.
  • A verifier. A function scoring whether $\hat{y}_N$ satisfies the problem's constraints. Sudoku: row / column / box violation penalty. Lean: the kernel's accept / reject verdict on the decoded proof, paired with a learned verifier head so the gradient flows back through $\hat{y}_N$ when the kernel is non-differentiable.

4. Deriving $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$, one line at a time.

Let $g(\hat{y}, \theta) := \nabla_{\hat{y}} E_\theta(x, \hat{y})$.

$$ \hat{y}_{k+1} \;=\; \hat{y}_k \;-\; \alpha\, g(\hat{y}_k, \theta) \tag{3} $$

Restate (1) using $g$.

$$ \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \;=\; \left(\frac{\partial J}{\partial \hat{y}_N}\right)^{\!\top}\,\frac{d \hat{y}_N}{d\theta} \tag{4} $$

Total derivative of (2). Only $\hat{y}_N$ depends on $\theta$.

$$ \frac{d \hat{y}_{k+1}}{d\theta} \;=\; \frac{d \hat{y}_k}{d\theta} \;-\; \alpha\!\left[\,\frac{\partial g}{\partial \hat{y}}\bigg|_{\hat{y}_k,\theta}\!\frac{d \hat{y}_k}{d\theta} \;+\; \frac{\partial g}{\partial \theta}\bigg|_{\hat{y}_k,\theta}\,\right] \tag{5} $$

Differentiate (3) w.r.t. $\theta$. $g$ depends on $\theta$ twice — through its second argument (explicit) and through $\hat{y}_k$ (implicit). Multivariate chain rule.

$$ H_k \,:=\, \nabla^2_{\hat{y}} E_\theta(x, \hat{y}_k) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}, \qquad M_k \,:=\, \nabla_\theta \nabla_{\hat{y}} E_\theta(x, \hat{y}_k) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d \times |\theta|} \tag{6} $$

Names. $H_k$ is the $\hat{y}$-Hessian of the energy at iterate $k$ — symmetric. $M_k$ is the mixed second derivative.

$$ \frac{d \hat{y}_{k+1}}{d\theta} \;=\; (I - \alpha H_k)\,\frac{d \hat{y}_k}{d\theta} \;-\; \alpha\, M_k \tag{7} $$

Substitute (6) into (5).

Unrolling (7) forward builds a $d \times |\theta|$ Jacobian — for any non-toy $|\theta|$, not stored. Solve backward.

$$ \lambda_k \,:=\, \left(\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d \hat{y}_k}\right)^{\!\top} \in\; \mathbb{R}^d \tag{8} $$

Definition: sensitivity of the loss to the $k$-th iterate.

$$ \lambda_N \;=\; \left(\frac{\partial J}{\partial \hat{y}_N}\right)^{\!\top} \tag{9} $$

Endpoint. For CE with softmax readout, $\lambda_N = \mathrm{softmax}(\hat{y}_N) - \mathbf{e}_y$. For MSE, $\lambda_N = 2(\hat{y}_N - y)$. The form of $J$ enters here and nowhere else.

$$ \frac{\partial \hat{y}_{k+1}}{\partial \hat{y}_k}\bigg|_{\theta \text{ fixed}} \;=\; I - \alpha H_k \tag{10} $$

Read from (3): hold $\theta$, differentiate in $\hat{y}_k$.

$$ \lambda_k \;=\; \lambda_{k+1} \;-\; \alpha\, H_k\, \lambda_{k+1} \tag{11} $$

Chain rule on (8) using (10); transpose; $H_k$ symmetric.

$$ \boxed{\;\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \;=\; -\,\alpha \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} M_k^\top\, \lambda_{k+1}\;} \tag{12} $$

Each step (3) injects a direct $\theta$-dependence equal to $-\alpha\, g(\hat{y}_k, \theta)$, whose Jacobian in $\theta$ (with $\hat{y}_k$ held fixed) is $-\alpha M_k$. Sum over steps; contract each with $\lambda_{k+1}$.

Two object types survive into the algorithm — both second-derivative-times-vector:

$$ H_k\, \lambda_{k+1} \;\;\text{(Hessian-vector product)} \qquad\qquad M_k^\top\, \lambda_{k+1} \;\;\text{(mixed Hessian-vector product)} \tag{13} $$

$H_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ with $d \sim 10^4$; $M_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times |\theta|}$ with $|\theta| \sim 10^9$. Neither matrix can be formed.

5. The Pearlmutter trick.

For any scalar $f(z)$ and any vector $v$ (treated as a constant):

$$ \big[\nabla^2_z f(z)\big]\, v \;=\; \nabla_z\!\left[\,(\nabla_z f(z))^\top\, v\,\right] \tag{14} $$

The right-hand side reads: one backward pass to get $\nabla_z f$; contract with $v$ to get a scalar; one more backward pass on that scalar. Two backward passes — roughly $2\times$ a single gradient evaluation — independent of $\dim(z)$. The Hessian is never materialized. The construction is from Pearlmutter (1994), Fast Exact Multiplication by the Hessian.

Sanity check on a quadratic.

Take $L(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2}\lVert A\theta - b \rVert^2$ with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, so $H = A^\top A$.

For $m \approx n$: $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ vs $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$. The general Pearlmutter result is the same kind of saving for any twice-differentiable $f$ — autodiff turns (14) into a one-liner:

# JAX
hvp = lambda f, z, v: jax.grad(lambda z: jnp.vdot(jax.grad(f)(z), v))(z)

# PyTorch
g   = torch.autograd.grad(f, z, create_graph=True)[0]
hvp = torch.autograd.grad(g, z, grad_outputs=v)[0]

Apply (14) with $f = E_\theta(x, \hat{y}_k)$ and $z = \hat{y}$ to get $H_k v$ in (11). Apply the same construction with the outer gradient taken w.r.t. $\theta$,

$$ M_k^\top v \;=\; \nabla_\theta\!\left[\,v^\top\, \nabla_{\hat{y}} E_\theta(x, \hat{y}_k)\,\right], $$

to get the mixed HVP in (12). Each is two backward passes. The full training step costs $\mathcal{O}(N)$ such pairs.

6. Recap.

  • §1. Autoregressive LLMs commit each token after one forward pass and cannot revise it.
  • §2. Energy-based alternative: minimize a learned verifier energy $E_\theta(x, \hat{y})$ over a continuous $\hat{y}$ — single token, full sequence, image latent, plan tensor. Inference is gradient descent on $E_\theta$ in $\hat{y}$-space; Langevin if you want sampling.
  • §3. Training: run (1) for $N$ steps, apply a loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = J(\hat{y}_N, y)$, backprop through every step. $J$ can be MSE / CE / Smooth-L1 — or a verifier (Sudoku constraint penalty, Lean kernel verdict).
  • §4. Unrolling the chain rule through (1) gives $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} = -\alpha \sum_k M_k^\top \lambda_{k+1}$, with adjoints $\lambda_k = \lambda_{k+1} - \alpha H_k \lambda_{k+1}$. Two second-derivative-times-vector objects appear: $H_k \lambda_{k+1}$ (HVP) and $M_k^\top \lambda_{k+1}$ (mixed HVP).
  • §5. Pearlmutter's identity $\nabla^2_z f \cdot v = \nabla_z[(\nabla_z f)^\top v]$ computes each HVP in two backward passes — independent of $\dim(z)$. Neither Hessian is ever formed.
